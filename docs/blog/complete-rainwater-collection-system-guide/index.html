The Complete Rainwater Collection System for Homes: Tanks, Filters & Diverters Explained

Published on 1/6/2025

This pillar guide walks you through every piece of a modern residential rainwater system—from gutters to storage, pumps, and treatment—so you can plan confidently and meet code.

System overview (from roof to tap)

  1. Catchment: Roof, gutters, downspouts.
  2. Pre‑screening: Leaf guards, basket strainers, first‑flush diverter.
  3. Conveyance: Piping to storage with unions for service and cleanouts.
  4. Storage: Above‑ground tanks or underground cisterns sized for your climate and demand.
  5. Treatment: Sediment + carbon filtration; UV or chlorine if potable or for whole‑house non‑potable.
  6. Distribution: Pump + pressure tank + controls; labeled non‑potable or potable per code.

Sizing the system

  • Annual capture estimate: Roof area (sq ft) × annual rainfall (in) × 0.62 ≈ gallons/year.
  • Practical storage: Size for dry spells (weeks, not months) unless you have extreme seasonality.
  • Use matching: Irrigation‑only systems can be smaller; whole‑house or fire reserve requires larger storage and robust controls.

Storage options

  • Above‑ground polyethylene: Economical, UV‑stabilized, easy to install; protect from sunlight and freezing.
  • Corrugated steel w/ liner: Scalable to large volumes; popular for whole‑house and farms.
  • Fiberglass: Durable, underground‑capable variants available.
  • Concrete cistern: Long life, underground, can buffer pH; heavier install.
  • Bladder tanks: Low‑profile/indoor crawlspace options.

Treatment and plumbing

  • Non‑potable (irrigation, toilets, laundry): Typically sediment + optional carbon, labeled piping, and backflow prevention.
  • Potable: Add disinfection (UV or chlorine). Include sample ports and maintenance log.
  • Whole‑house filtration options: /blog/ultimate-guide-whole-house-water-filtration

Controls and safety

  • Pump protection: Dry‑run sensors and low‑level cutoffs.
  • Municipal backup: Air‑gap or break tank; never hard‑connect pressurized city water to storage without proper backflow protection.
  • Overflow: Screened, directed away from foundations or to landscaping.

Code and permitting

  • When permits apply: Underground tanks, electrical for pumps, plumbing tie‑ins, trenching.
  • Labeling: “Non‑potable water—Do Not Drink” where required.
  • Local rules vary—start with your city building department and state plumbing/health code.
